Phase 1: Foundations

The building blocks of price action trading.

01
Beginner
What is Price Action?
Price action means the market's actual movement reflects all available information.
  • Price reflects everything — every fundamental, sentiment, and order flow event is already in the bar
  • Indicators are derivatives — they are calculated from price; price action reads the source directly
  • All traders see the same chart — price action setups work because enough participants act on the same signals
  • Brooks' core belief: the market is mostly random but has brief periods of non-randomness that can be traded
The market is always in breakout mode or trading range mode, and you need to know which before you place a trade.— Al Brooks
02
Beginner
Reading Individual Bars
Every candle has OHLC. The relationship between these tells you who won the battle between buyers and sellers.
  • Open, high, low, close — each component reveals who won control during that period
  • Bull bar: close above open. Bear bar: close below open. Doji: indecision
  • Wick analysis: large upper wick = sellers rejected high; large lower wick = buyers rejected low
  • Bar size matters — a large-bodied bar shows conviction; a small body shows uncertainty
  • The close is most important — where price closes within the bar reveals who 'won' that bar
03
Beginner
Signal Bars & Follow-Through
A signal bar shows potential entry. The next bar (follow-through) confirms if the setup is real or a trap.
  • Strong signal bar: large body, closes near high (bull) or low (bear), small wicks, opens near opposite end
  • Weak signal bar: large wicks, small body, closes in the middle — low confidence setup
  • Entry bar = the bar you actually enter on. Setup bar = the bar before that confirmed the pattern
  • Follow-through — a strong close on the entry bar confirms momentum. Weak follow-through = consider exiting early
04
Beginner
Timeframes & Context
Brooks' default: 5-minute ES chart. Higher timeframe context is mandatory.
  • Brooks' default: 5-minute ES chart — most of his examples and rules are calibrated to this
  • Higher timeframe context is mandatory — a buy signal on 5-min inside a bear trend on daily is low probability
  • Every bar on a higher TF = a pattern on a lower TF — zooming in reveals the internal structure
  • Do not over-chart — Brooks recommends mastering one chart before adding more timeframes
05
Beginner
Support & Resistance
Prior swing high/low = most reliable S/R. The market has memory at these levels.
  • Prior swing high/low = most reliable S/R. The market has memory at these levels
  • Prior day high, low, close — institutional levels that many algorithms reference
  • S/R becomes opposite — broken support becomes resistance (and vice versa). Watch for the retest
  • Exact vs approximate: Brooks doesn't use precise levels — he uses zones and expects noise around them
06
Beginner
VWAP — Volume Weighted Average Price
VWAP shows where institutions are accumulating. Price respects it like a magnet.
  • Above VWAP = bullish bias. Below VWAP = bearish bias
  • Touch VWAP = test level — watch for rejection or acceptance
  • VWAP is institutional — algorithms reference it for execution quality
  • VWAP bands (standard deviation) act as dynamic S/R

Phase 2: Market Structure

Understanding the bigger picture.

07
Intermediate
Trend Days vs Range Days
Trend day: opens near one extreme, closes near the other. Range day: price oscillates.
  • Trend day: opens near one extreme, closes near the other. Pullbacks are shallow and brief
  • Range day: price oscillates between a high and low with no dominant direction. Fade the extremes
  • Trading range day strategy: buy low, sell high. Never chase breakouts on range days until confirmed
  • Trend day strategy: buy every pullback, never fade. Exit at measured move targets or EOD
08
Intermediate
Bull & Bear Trends
HH/HL structure = bull trend. LL/LH structure = bear trend.
  • HH/HL structure = bull trend. LL/LH structure = bear trend. Breaking this structure signals reversal
  • Strong trend: consecutive bull bars, small pullbacks, bars close near high, little overlap between bars
  • Weak trend: overlapping bars, large wicks against trend, deep pullbacks — consider reducing size
  • Always-in-long/short: Brooks asks 'if forced to be in the market, which way?' — this is your bias
09
Intermediate
Trading Ranges
Price oscillates between support and resistance. The market spends ~70% of time in ranges.
  • Definition: price oscillates between support and resistance without a clear directional bias
  • Bull trap / bear trap: breakouts from ranges fail 80% of the time in the first attempt
  • Tight trading range (TTR): bars overlap heavily — extremely low probability for both sides
  • Strategy: fade the extremes (buy bottom third, sell top third) and scale out at the midpoint
  • Wait for the second breakout — the first attempt out of a range usually fails and reverses back inside
10
Intermediate
Measured Moves & Legs
Two-legged pullback: the most reliable pullback structure.
  • Two-legged pullback: the most reliable pullback structure — market probes lower, bounces, probes again, then resumes trend
  • Measured move (MM): project the height of leg 1 from the start of leg 2 to get a target for leg 2
  • Three pushes: three legs in the same direction often mark exhaustion — expect reversal or range
  • Swing size matters: small legs = tight market; large legs = trending. Match position size to volatility
11
Intermediate
Trend Lines & Channels
Trend line: drawn through swing lows in an uptrend. A break signals potential reversal.
  • Trend line: drawn through swing lows in an uptrend (or highs in downtrend). A break signals potential reversal
  • Channel: parallel line above/below the trend line creates a channel. Price often touches both walls
  • Micro trend lines: steep short-term channels during strong momentum phases — breaks signal pauses
  • Overshoot and reversal: price often breaks a channel slightly before reversing back inside

Phase 3: Entry Setups

The specific setups to trade.

12
Intermediate
M2B: Moving Average 2nd Entry Long
Two-legged pullback down to 20 EMA. Buy the 2nd touch.
  • Uptrend required — series of higher highs and higher lows. Price predominantly above EMA
  • Two-legged pullback down to the EMA. First leg bounces, second leg tests the EMA again
  • Entry: Buy stop 1 tick above the high of the bar that touches the EMA on the 2nd leg down
  • Stop: 1 tick below the low of the 2nd leg pullback
  • Do not enter on the first leg — wait for the second test to confirm the level is holding
13
Intermediate
M2S: Moving Average 2nd Entry Short
Downtrend. Two-legged rally up to 20 EMA. Short the 2nd touch.
  • Downtrend required — series of lower lows and lower highs. Price predominantly below EMA
  • Two-legged rally up to the EMA. First leg bounces, second leg tests the EMA again
  • Entry: Sell stop 1 tick below the low of the bar that touches the EMA on the 2nd leg up
  • Stop: 1 tick above the high of the 2nd leg rally
  • Institutions sell rallies to the EMA in downtrends — the second touch traps weak shorts
14
Intermediate
Inside Bar Pattern (IB)
Mother bar sets range. Inside bars compress. Breakout of inside bar is the trigger.
  • Definition: high ≤ prior high AND low ≥ prior low. The market paused and compressed
  • B1 Mother Bar — wide range, sets boundaries. B2-B3 — compression inside B1
  • Breakout entry: buy 1 tick above the inside bar high or sell 1 tick below the low
  • Context is everything — inside bar after a strong bull bar = continuation. After a weak bar = neutral
  • ioi (inside-outside-inside): a pattern of compression-expansion-compression that often precedes large moves
15
Intermediate
Gap & Go
Large gap on open + holds direction + volume = trade the gap.
  • Gap up = initial bullish bias — but watch if the gap fills in the first 30 min. A gap fill = bear strength
  • Gap bar (first bar of day): if it's a strong bull bar, buy on a pullback. If bear bar, consider shorts
  • Opening reversal: if the first bar reverses strongly, a reversal is in play
  • First-hour range: often sets the framework for the rest of the day — respect its high and low as key levels
16
Advanced
Failed Breakouts & Traps
Bull trap: price breaks above resistance, fails to follow through, closes back below.
  • Bull trap: price breaks above resistance, fails to follow through, closes back below. Shorts trigger as trapped longs exit
  • Bear trap: breaks below support, immediately reverses — trapped shorts add fuel to the rally
  • Entry timing: enter when price closes back through the broken level, or on the first pullback after reversal
  • High probability context: failed breakouts out of trading ranges fail ~80% of the time on the first attempt
17
Advanced
Pullback Entries (2nd Entry)
Two-legged pullback = highest probability.
  • Two-legged pullback = highest probability. The market tested the pullback level twice before resuming
  • Entry: buy above a bull reversal bar at the end of the pullback, stop below the pullback low
  • Target: prior trend high, measured move, or next major resistance
  • Never chase: if the pullback entry bar is too large, wait for the next pullback — don't buy into strength
18
Advanced
Breakout Pullback Entry
Price breaks resistance → pullback to retest → continuation higher.
  • Pattern: price breaks resistance → pullback to retest that level (now support) → continuation higher
  • Entry: buy the first pullback after a genuine breakout. Stop below the new support level
  • Confirmation: the pullback should be orderly (bear bars are small), not panic selling
  • Target: measured move (height of the base projected from the breakout point)
19
Advanced
Trend Reversals
Step 1: break of the trend line. Step 2: lower high or higher low. Step 3: break of the most recent swing.
  • Step 1: break of the trend line. Necessary but not sufficient on its own
  • Step 2: formation of a lower high (in uptrend) or higher low (in downtrend) — the trend structure is damaged
  • Step 3: break below the most recent swing low (uptrend) or above swing high (downtrend)
  • Brooks' warning: the first reversal attempt fails ~60-70% of the time. Wait for the second signal
20
Advanced
Wedge Patterns
Three pushes in one direction = exhaustion.
  • Wedge bull flag: three lower lows, each declining less — buyers are absorbing pressure. Expect upward breakout
  • Wedge bear flag: three higher highs, each rising less — sellers absorbing. Expect downward breakout
  • Expanding wedge: each push exceeds the prior — volatile, difficult to trade. Often resolves in a range
  • Target after wedge: measured move back to the start of the three-push structure

Phase 4: Risk Management

Preserving capital.

21
Intermediate
Stop Placement Rules
Default stop: 1 tick beyond the signal bar's extreme.
  • Default stop: 1 tick beyond the signal bar's extreme (high for shorts, low for longs)
  • Wider stop for context: if the setup bar is large, stop goes beyond the setup bar (not signal bar)
  • Never widen a stop — if price is moving toward your stop, the trade is wrong. Take the loss
  • Move stop to breakeven once a scalp profit is secured on part of the position
22
Intermediate
Risk:Reward Ratios
Minimum R:R = 1:1. Ideal R:R = 2:1 or better.
  • Minimum R:R = 1:1. If your stop is 4 pts, your target should be at least 4 pts
  • Ideal R:R = 2:1 or better. Take setups where the measured move target is at least twice the stop distance
  • Avoid 1:2 setups — risking 4 pts to make 2 pts requires a >67% win rate just to break even
  • The math: 50% win rate with 1.5:1 R:R = profitable. 60% win rate with 0.8:1 R:R = unprofitable
23
Intermediate
Position Sizing
Risk 1-2% of account per trade.
  • Risk 1-2% of account per trade
  • $10k account = 1 MES ($100-200 risk)
  • $25k account = 1 ES ($250-500 risk)
  • $50k account = 2 ES ($500-1000 risk)
  • Contracts = (Account × 0.01) ÷ (Stop in pts × $50)
24
Intermediate
The Kill Switch
When down 15 pts on the day, STOP TRADING.
  • Daily Loss Limit: Stop trading if down 15 pts on the day. No matter what you think you see
  • Consecutive Losses: After 2 losing trades back-to-back, take a 60-min break before any new trade
  • Weekly Cap: If down 40 pts in a week, stop for the rest of that week
  • No revenge trading: Every trade stands alone. A loss is information — not a score to settle

Phase 5: Market Conditions

Reading the environment.

25
Advanced
VIX & Market Volatility
VIX < 14 = quiet, tight ranges. VIX > 18 = volatile, wide swings.
  • VIX < 14 = quiet, tight ranges. Reduce size or sit out
  • VIX > 18 = volatile, wide swings. Be cautious with entries
  • VIX > 40 = fear mode — erratic moves, wide spreads. Sit out or cut size in half
  • News catalyst = wider ranges. Adjust stops and targets accordingly
26
Advanced
FOMC, CPI, NFP Events
Never trade 30 minutes before/after major news releases.
  • Avoid 30 min before/after FOMC, CPI, NFP releases
  • Wide spreads on news — you will get slipped on entries and exits
  • Sit out and preserve capital — there are always other setups
  • After the initial reaction, wait for clear structure to form before trading
27
Advanced
Gap Analysis
Overnight gaps > 30 pts suggest trend day. Gaps < 10 pts suggest range day.
  • Globex gap > 30 pts = likely trend day — trade with direction
  • Globex gap < 10 pts = likely range day — fade extremes
  • Gap fill in first 30 min = bear strength (for gap ups)
  • Use for morning bias before the open
28
Advanced
Session Management
RTH = 9:30-4:00 ET. Best liquidity 9:30-12:00 and 2:00-3:30.
  • RTH (Regular Trading Hours) = 9:30-4:00 ET
  • Best liquidity: 9:30-12:00 and 2:00-3:30
  • Lunch hour (12:00-2:00) = choppy, skip it
  • No new trades after 3:30 PM — close all positions before close

Phase 6: Psychology & Execution

The mental game.

29
Advanced
The Three Disciplines
Entry, exit, and risk discipline.
  • Entry discipline — only take setups on your checklist. No 'gut feel' trades
  • Exit discipline — take profit at targets, not emotion. Don't let winners turn into losers
  • Risk discipline — follow position sizing rules. Never increase size after a win or decrease after a loss
30
Advanced
Revenge Trading
After a loss, the urge to 'make it back' is the #1 account killer.
  • After loss = take break. Don't add to losers
  • Don't add to losers — ever. Each trade stands alone
  • Let next setup come naturally. The market will be there tomorrow
  • The urge to 'make it back' is the #1 account killer
31
Advanced
Overtrading
Max 4 trades/day. Quality > quantity.
  • Max 4 trades/day. No exceptions
  • Quality > quantity. Each trade must pass your checklist
  • If you're taking 7-8 trades, you're chasing — not trading
  • Fewer, higher-quality trades = better results
32
Advanced
Keeping a Trading Journal
Write down every trade — entry reason, exit reason, what you learned.
  • Every trade logged — entry reason, exit reason, emotional state
  • Why you entered. Why you exited. What you'd do different
  • No cherry-picking. Log losses too
  • Review weekly. Patterns emerge that you can't see day-to-day
33
Advanced
Consistency Over Perfection
55% win rate with proper R:R = profitable.
  • 55% win rate with proper R:R = profitable
  • Follow the process. Results follow process
  • A losing trade executed correctly is a good trade
  • A winning trade on a bad setup is a bad trade
Most traders lose money. The ones who win are those who accept that they will be wrong 40–50% of the time and still execute their plan without emotion.— Al Brooks
34
Advanced
The Long Road
5 years minimum — Brooks consistently says it takes 5+ years of serious study.
  • 5 years minimum — Brooks consistently says it takes 5+ years of serious study
  • Study 5,000+ charts — there is no substitute for screen time. Pattern recognition is built, not read
  • Start with simulation — trade in simulation for at least 6 months before risking real capital
  • Accept uncertainty — no setup is guaranteed. You are playing probabilities, not predicting
